# Meditation entries (Aim + Detailed instructions + Reflection)

Source: `merge/meditation_mobile_10_minimal.html` (`RAW_TEXT`)

## 1. Anapanasati

_Build one small, exact breath object at the nostrils or upper lip. Counting is scaffolding, not the practice itself._

### Aim

build a small, exact, repeatable breath object at the nostrils or upper lip so the mind stops sliding all over the body.

### Detailed instructions

- Sit in a posture you can actually hold without constant adjustment. Keep the body upright but not rigid. Let the face, jaw, shoulders, and belly settle.
- Choose one exact touch point where the breath is most obvious: just below the nostrils, at the nostril rims, or on the upper lip. Do not keep changing locations mid-sit.
- Know only the touch of the in-breath and out-breath at that point. Do not follow the breath into the chest, abdomen, throat, or outside into space.
- For now, keep the breath as a simple meditation object. Do not start investigating impermanence, non-self, or the elements in the breath itself. The job here is steadiness.
- If the mind keeps slipping, count after each full breath cycle: "in, out, one ... in, out, eight." Reset when lost. Count calmly; do not chant at yourself.
- When attention wanders, return without drama. The practice is the return itself repeated cleanly, not the fantasy of never getting distracted.

### Reflection

- Good practice: the target zone gets smaller and clearer; breathing stays natural; counting supports attention instead of dominating it.
- Bad practice: chasing the breath through the body, controlling the breathing, widening the object to the whole face, or adding lots of inner talk.
- Advance when: you can stay with the contact point for a good stretch without constantly forgetting it, and counting begins to feel optional rather than necessary.

## 2. Long / short

_Shift from counting to direct knowing of the breath's duration, while keeping the same tiny contact point._

### Aim

shift from counting to direct knowing of the breath's duration while keeping the same small contact point.

### Detailed instructions

- Drop the count when the mind can already stay near the breath. Stay at the same touch point you established in Meditation 1.
- Decide for yourself what counts as long and what counts as short. In this text, long and short refer to time duration, not literal size.
- Know the breath by the duration of its touch at the nostrils or upper lip. You are not measuring it in inches or trying to make it dramatic.
- Keep the mental note minimal. A light recognition is enough. The more you turn this into commentary, the more you lose the object.
- Let some breaths be long and some short. Do not secretly start breathing in a special way just to make the exercise easier.

### Reflection

- Good practice: recognition of long or short happens almost in the background while attention remains stable on contact.
- Bad practice: lengthening the breath on purpose, over-labelling each breath, or drifting away from the nostrils into abstract thinking about "breath patterns."
- Advance when: you can know duration cleanly without losing the touch point and without needing to steer the breathing.

## 3. Whole breath

_Know each whole in-breath and out-breath continuously. Let the breath become subtle without panicking or meddling._

### Aim

continuous awareness of each whole in-breath and out-breath, followed by a calmer and subtler breath that does not throw you off the object.

### Detailed instructions

- Stay with the same touch point but now know the whole in-breath from beginning to end and the whole out-breath from beginning to end. The continuity matters more than verbal clarity.
- If an image or sign starts to appear, do not immediately jump to it. Keep knowing the breath until the mind is genuinely steady enough that the sign is stable on its own.
- To calm the breath, make a gentle internal decision that the breathing should settle. Then do nothing else. The text is explicit: if you meddle and force calmness, concentration breaks.
- When the breath becomes very fine, do not react by gasping, deepening the breath, or checking whether you are still breathing. Keep awareness where the breath was last clearly known and wait there.
- Use the source's hard correction: you are not one of the rare beings who are not breathing. The problem is not absence of breath; the problem is that mindfulness is not yet sharp enough to catch its subtlety.

### Reflection

- Good practice: the breath gets softer while the mind gets more patient, more still, and less busy.
- Bad practice: forcing the breath to be obvious, getting excited about early signs, or treating subtlety as danger.
- Advance when: subtle breathing no longer scares or frustrates you, and continuity at the touch point is stronger than the urge to interfere.

## 4. Air (pushing)

_Shift from one breath point to the bodily quality of pushing, pressure, movement, or thrust._

### Aim

shift from a single breath point to a bodily quality that can be found again and again through direct touch-based observation.

### Detailed instructions

- Use the steadier mind developed with breathing and start looking for pushing, the first characteristic commonly taught in the detailed four-elements method.
- Begin where it is easiest: the pushing in the centre of the head with breathing, the expansion of the chest, the movement of the abdomen, or even the pulse beat. Start where the signal is real, not where you think it should be impressive.
- Stay with one location until pushing is clear. Then move to a nearby area and look again. Expand methodically: head, neck, trunk, arms, legs, feet.
- Do not rush to "the whole body" after one decent moment. The point is to train recognition so that wherever awareness lands, pushing can be found there too.
- Whenever you are unsure, ask a simple question: is there actual tactile movement, pressure, or thrust here, or am I only naming a body part from memory?

### Reflection

- Good practice: pushing becomes easier to rediscover in both obvious and subtle places, and you rely on touch rather than imagination.
- Bad practice: making a vague body sweep and calling it "energy," or insisting on exotic sensations instead of the plain tactile quality the text is pointing to.
- Advance when: pushing can be found through much of the body without strain, even when it is quiet rather than dramatic.

## 5. Earth (tactile)

_Build the earth-element through clear tactile qualities instead of a vague sense of "the body."_

### Aim

make the first three earth qualities distinct enough that the body starts being known as qualities instead of as a lump called "me."

### Detailed instructions

- Hardness: bite the teeth together lightly, then relax the bite and keep noticing the hardness that remains. After that, scan for hardness through the body without deliberately tensing anything.
- Roughness: rub the tongue over the edge of the teeth or brush a hand over the skin. Once the quality is recognized locally, go looking for it elsewhere in the body.
- Heaviness: place one hand on the other and feel the weight of the upper hand, or bend the head slightly forward and notice its heaviness. Then extend that recognition through the body.
- Alternate these with pushing: pushing -> hardness -> roughness -> heaviness. Repetition matters. The text is not asking for one memorable insight but for trained discernment.
- Keep the scan systematic. Head to feet is better than random hopping, because random hopping usually means the mind is already slipping back into vagueness.

### Reflection

- Good practice: the qualities stop collapsing into one mushy "body feeling" and become discriminable.
- Bad practice: clenching muscles to manufacture hardness, scratching around for roughness, or turning the exercise into concept review instead of perception.
- Advance when: you can run these four qualities--pushing, hardness, roughness, heaviness--through the body with real confidence.

## 6. Balance (opposites)

_Complete the easier tactile set and learn to correct excess by using opposite qualities._

### Aim

complete the easier tactile set and learn how opposite qualities stop the practice from turning harsh, tense, or one-sided.

### Detailed instructions

- Supporting: briefly let the body slump forward, then straighten it again. The force keeping the body upright, steady, and erect is supporting. Discern that force throughout the body.
- Softness: press the tongue against the inside of the lip and recognize softness there. Then look for softness elsewhere without collapsing posture.
- Smoothness: wet the lips and rub the tongue across them to recognize smoothness, then expand the search to the rest of the body.
- Lightness: wag one finger up and down and notice its lightness. Again, do not stop there; train body-wide recognition.
- Now cycle eight qualities in sequence. If the body starts feeling too hard, too rough, or too pressured, use the opposite quality deliberately. This is not decorative theory; it is practical correction.

### Reflection

- Good practice: you can lighten, soften, or steady the practice by choosing the appropriate opposite instead of just forcing more effort.
- Bad practice: treating supporting as muscular bracing, or trying to hold all qualities at once until the whole exercise becomes muddy.
- Advance when: the first eight qualities are available across the body and using opposites actually rebalances strain or excess.

## 7. Fire / water

_Complete the twelve qualities with heat, coldness, cohesion, and flowing. The last two are subtler and often inferential._

### Aim

finish the twelve qualities and understand that not every quality is known in exactly the same way.

### Detailed instructions

- Heat: start with warmth anywhere obvious in the body. Usually this one is straightforward.
- Coldness: begin with the coolness of the breath entering the nostrils, then extend that recognition more broadly.
- Cohesion: notice how skin, flesh, and sinews hold the body together. The text gives the blunt image: without cohesion, the body would fall into separate pieces. This one is known by inference from the functioning of the body.
- Flowing: use the movement of saliva, blood, air into the lungs, or heat spreading through the body. Again, this is subtler and often known inferentially.
- If cohesion or flowing are vague, go back to the previous ten qualities. The text explicitly says the last two become clearer when the earlier ones are firm. For cohesion, pushing and hardness are especially useful supports; for flowing, coldness, heat, or pushing can help.

### Reflection

- Good practice: you understand that the last two qualities are subtler and do not demand crude liquid sensations before allowing them to count.
- Bad practice: inventing imagery, forcing anatomical fantasies, or deciding the practice failed because cohesion was not tactile in the same way as hardness.
- Advance when: all twelve qualities can be found clearly enough that you can work with the whole set instead of a handful of favorites.

## 8. Four elements (whole)

_Regroup the twelve qualities into the four elements and hold the body in one cleaner overall view._

### Aim

move from sequential body scanning into a calmer whole-body overview where the body is known as earth, water, fire, and air.

### Detailed instructions

- Regroup the twelve qualities: the first six as earth, the next two as water, the next two as fire, and the last two as air.
- Take the whole body at one glance instead of hopping part by part. The text suggests the overview is often easiest as if looking from behind the shoulders.
- Discern the elements in order: earth -> water -> fire -> air. Go neither too fast nor too slow. Too fast means nothing is really clear; too slow means the practice loses continuity.
- Go beyond concepts. Do not merely recite "earth, water, fire, air." Stay with the actual qualities they refer to.
- If a characteristic is temporarily unclear, you may leave it out provided you still keep at least one quality for each element and do not create a painful imbalance by doing so.
- Use the balancing instructions from the faculties and enlightenment factors if the mind becomes dull, overexcited, lazy, or agitated.

### Reflection

- Good practice: the body starts being perceived less as a personal shape and more as a field of knowable qualities.
- Bad practice: speed-reciting the elements, chasing special effects, or dropping back into random body scanning every few seconds.
- Advance when: the four-element overview is steady enough that repeated rounds can be done cleanly and relatively quickly without losing clarity.

## 9. Light / transparency

_Treat light and transparency as objects for further discernment, not as fireworks or attainment badges._

### Aim

treat changes in light and transparency as objects for further discernment, not as flashy attainments in themselves.

### Detailed instructions

- As concentration matures, different kinds of light may appear. The text describes a progression for some meditators from smoky grey to whiter light, then bright white, then a body appearing as a white form.
- Do not get hypnotized by the visual aspect. Keep discerning the four elements in that light or white form.
- If the form becomes transparent, like ice or glass, continue discerning elements there too. At this stage the body transparent-element is being picked up more clearly.
- When that transparent form sparkles and emits light, sustained concentration on it may mark access concentration. Use that as a stable platform, not as a trophy.
- Then look for space-element within the transparent form. The source says the block breaks into small particles called rupa kalapas.
- Your first job is not "instant insight." Your first job is to discern the four elements in transparent and opaque kalapas. If arising and passing are too fast, ignore that for now and stay with the elemental analysis.

### Reflection

- Good practice: light and transparency make you more precise, not more grandiose.
- Bad practice: treating lights as proof of enlightenment, forcing visual effects, or thinking a blank discontinuity must be Nibbana.
- Advance when: you can return to this stage steadily and start distinguishing transparent from opaque groupings without losing composure.

## 10. Materiality (rupa)

_Use theory to sharpen direct seeing, not to replace it with memorized taxonomy._

### Aim

let the theory sections clarify direct seeing rather than replacing it with classification collecting.

### Detailed instructions

- Start with the text's basic claim that each rupa kalapa contains at least eight kinds of materiality: earth, water, fire, air, colour, odour, taste, and nutritive-essence.
- Then begin distinguishing transparent-elements by what object impinges on them: colour for the eye, touch for the body, sound for the ear, odour for the nose, taste for the tongue.
- Work one base at a time. Do not begin with the twenty-eight types as a giant memorization task. First understand what "materiality" is doing in this book and why transparent and opaque groupings matter.
- Only then use the master map of twenty-eight materialities so the taxonomy explains what you are looking at rather than burying you in names.
- If perception becomes merely conceptual, step back to the whole-body four-elements overview or to the transparent/opaque kalapa work. The later lists only help when they are anchored in actual discernment.
- Reality check: this stage is only a beginning. The text itself keeps warning that merely seeing particles or memorizing categories is not enough. Deeper analysis, causes, compactness, and later insight work still have to be done carefully.

### Reflection

- Good practice: theory sharpens observation and observation keeps theory honest.
- Bad practice: opening the big taxonomies too early and replacing discernment with intellectual collection.
- Advance when: the links between direct bodily observation, transparent-elements, kalapas, and the theory sections are becoming intelligible rather than random.
